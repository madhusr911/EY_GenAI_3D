Why Split the Work at All?
One agent with many tools (M3.1) still works. So why bother with a team?

😵 One generalist, doing everything

One prompt, one set of instructions, juggling calculator questions AND policy questions AND anything else you add later. Every new skill makes the instructions longer and the agent's job less focused.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 A small team of specialists

A calculation specialist that only ever does maths. A research specialist that only ever searches policy. Each one's instructions stay short and focused — and a supervisor decides who's needed.

📐 Today's plain-English idea

A supervisor is just a manager who doesn't do the work — it reads the question and decides which specialist(s) should. This is a hierarchy: one supervisor, two specialists, exactly like a small team at Meridian Bank.

🤔 Quick guess: does the supervisor need its own tools, like a calculator?
No — that's the whole point. The supervisor's only job is deciding WHO answers. The specialists own the actual tools. Mixing the two back together would just recreate M3.1's one-generalist problem.

Concept B
A Hand-off Is a Focused Instruction, Not the Whole Conversation
The supervisor doesn't just forward the customer's raw message. It writes each specialist a short, focused instruction.

❌ Forwarding the raw message

"Will my ₹1,25,000 UPI transfer clear, and what's 2% cashback on it?" — sent whole to the calculator specialist, who now has to figure out which part is even its job.

✅ A focused hand-off

Calculator specialist gets: "What is 2% of ₹1,25,000?" Research specialist gets: "What is Meridian's daily UPI limit?" Each gets only what it needs.

This is exactly like a support call being transferred with a one-line summary — not a rerun of the entire call from the start. The receiving specialist gets a clean, relevant instruction, not a transcript to untangle.

🤔 Why bother writing a focused instruction instead of just forwarding everything?
A specialist with a focused instruction is faster and less likely to get confused by irrelevant parts of the original question. This is the same "match the tool to the job" idea from M3.1's Concept D, one level up.

Concept C
What Each Specialist Sees — and What It Doesn't
Isolated state: each specialist only ever sees its own hand-off. Not the other specialist's answer. Not the full conversation.

🧮 Calculation Specialist sees

"What is 2% of ₹1,25,000?"

Nothing about UPI limits. Nothing about the research specialist.

🔍 Research Specialist sees

"What is Meridian's daily UPI limit?"

Nothing about cashback. Nothing about the calculator's answer.

This is a deliberate design choice, not an accident. Keeping each specialist's world small means it can't get distracted by irrelevant context, and a mistake in one specialist can't quietly leak into the other's answer. The supervisor is the only thing that sees both results — that's where they get combined.

🤔 If the research specialist somehow got confused, would that affect the calculator specialist's answer?
B is right! That isolation is the whole benefit — one specialist's mistake stays contained.

Concept D
More Agents, More Calls, More Cost
A team is more focused than one generalist. It also isn't free. Real numbers below.

💰 One real example (gpt-4o-mini rates, July 2026 — re-check before class if pricing may have changed)

ApproachLLM callsApprox. cost per question
One generalist agent, both tools1~$0.00015
Supervisor + 2 specialists (today's pattern)3~$0.00027

Roughly 1.8× the cost, for a question that genuinely needs two kinds of expertise. At Meridian's real query volume, that difference is worth knowing before you add a fourth specialist "just in case."

✅ What you buy with that extra cost

Each specialist stays focused and easier to trust — the same reliability argument from M3.1's "when NOT to use an agent," one level up: more agents only where the extra focus is worth paying for.

⚠️ What you're trading away

More calls, more cost, and — if you call them one after another — more time. That last part is exactly what Concept E fixes.

Concept E · Industry Best Practice
Why Wait? Run Them at the Same Time
When two specialists don't depend on each other's answer, there's no reason to make one wait for the other.

🍳 Mental model: two chefs, two dishes

If neither dish depends on the other, you don't cook them one after another — you start both at once. The UPI-limit lookup and the cashback calculation don't depend on each other either. Calling them one after another is a habit, not a requirement.
